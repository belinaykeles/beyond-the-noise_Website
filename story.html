The Story

How space, interaction, and vulnerability shape traffic risk in New York City.

Geographic Total Collision Heatmap

Thousands of traffic collisions are recorded across New York City every year. When these incidents are placed back onto the map, they stop appearing random. Some areas consistently absorb more crashes than others, reflecting how movement, density, and urban structure concentrate risk.

High-density corridors in Brooklyn and Queens emerge as the primary peaks for total collision volume. In contrast, Staten Island consistently maintains the lowest frequency of incidents, benefiting from lower population density and different urban transit patterns.

Vehicle & Factor Risk Matrix

Collision concentration alone does not explain why some incidents result in injuries while others do not.

By examining vehicle types together with reported contributing factors, this matrix highlights combinations that repeatedly align with higher casualty involvement. Each cell summarizes an average risk pattern drawn from historical data, allowing structural signals to emerge from routine traffic noise.

The peak risk of casualty is observed in the interaction between Two-Wheeled and Impairment, where outcomes are most severe. The lowest average risk cells are typically occupied by Trucks or Commercial Cars involved in Vehicle Defect.

Urban Vulnerability Distribution

Risk is not experienced equally by all road users. A collision that leaves a motorist unharmed can be life-altering for a pedestrian or cyclist. This view shifts the focus from vehicles to people, revealing how casualty burden is unevenly distributed across road-user groups and boroughs.

Brooklyn and Queens represent the peaks for total pedestrian and cyclist casualties, highlighting significant infrastructure challenges. Conversely, the lowest casualty rates for vulnerable road users are found in Staten Island, though the casualty per incident remains a concern.

Factor Association Overview

To further contextualize these patterns, key variables are summarized through relative association measures.

Odds ratios provide a comparative view of how selected factors align with changes in casualty likelihood within the observed data, complementing the earlier spatial and distributional analyses.

Two-Wheeled vehicles represent the absolute peak in casualty association, showing an odds ratio significantly higher than any other vehicle type. Among specific contributing factors, Impairment and Environmental conditions show the highest positive association with injuries, while Human Error and Unspecified factors align with the lowest relative odds of casualty in this model.

Conclusion

Seen together, these charts tell a connected story. Traffic risk in New York City does not arise from isolated events, but from the interaction between where movement concentrates, how situations unfold, and who remains most exposed.

By tracing these patterns across space, conditions, and road users, the analysis moves beyond individual incidents toward a clearer understanding of how urban traffic risk takes shape.
